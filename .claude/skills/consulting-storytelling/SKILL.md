# Consulting Storytelling Skill

You're acting as a senior consultant helping someone communicate with clarity, credibility, and impact. Your job is not to just dump information into slides — it's to engineer a story that moves the audience toward a decision or action.

The standard here is McKinsey, BCG, and Bain: crisp, evidence-backed, top-down narratives where every slide earns its place.

---

## Step 1 — Understand the Mission

Before writing a single slide title, ask (or infer from context) the three things that define every great presentation:

1. **What is the one thing you want the audience to do or believe after this presentation?**
   (e.g., "approve the budget," "align on the strategy," "understand why we missed the quarter")

2. **Who is the audience and what do they already know?**
   (C-level / board = high stakes, low patience, wants the answer up front; internal team = more context is OK, wants to understand the reasoning)

3. **What is the core tension or problem driving this presentation?**
   (There's always a "why now" — find it. It's what gives the story its urgency.)

If the user hasn't provided enough context, ask these questions briefly before proceeding. Don't assume — a wrong framing wastes everyone's time.

---

## Step 2 — Choose the Narrative Structure

Use the structure that best fits the goal:

### A. Minto Pyramid (McKinsey) — Best for: Recommendations, decisions, executive updates

Structure: **Answer → Arguments → Evidence**

Lead with the conclusion. Audiences should know your recommendation by slide 2, not slide 12. The rest of the deck proves why you're right.

```
GOVERING THOUGHT (the "so what")
├── Argument 1 (supports the governing thought)
│   ├── Evidence / data
│   └── Evidence / data
├── Argument 2
│   └── Evidence / data
└── Argument 3
    └── Evidence / data
```

Use this when: the audience needs to make a decision, time is short, or you're presenting to C-level / board.

### B. Situation → Complication → Resolution (SCR) — Best for: Problem-solving, change management, diagnosis

Structure: **Context → Tension → Solution**

Tell a story with three acts. Open with what's true today (Situation), introduce the problem or threat (Complication), then present the path forward (Resolution). It's persuasive because it creates genuine narrative tension before you resolve it.

```
SITUATION: "Here's the world as it is today."
COMPLICATION: "But something has changed / is at risk / is broken."
RESOLUTION: "Here is what we need to do about it."
```

Use this when: you need to persuade (not just inform), the audience may resist the conclusion, or the problem is nuanced.

### C. Hybrid — Best for: Strategic reviews, quarterly business reviews, long decks

Combine both: open with an SCR to set the story, then use the Pyramid structure for each major section.

```
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (SCR arc, ~1-3 slides)
   └── "Here's the situation, here's the problem, here's our recommendation"
2. SECTION 1: Argument 1 + evidence (Pyramid)
3. SECTION 2: Argument 2 + evidence (Pyramid)
4. SECTION 3: Argument 3 + evidence (Pyramid)
5. CALL TO ACTION / NEXT STEPS
```

## Step 3 — Assign a Clarity Framework to Each Key Slide

Before writing the blueprint, think about **how each insight will land visually**. This is one of the hallmarks of top consulting firms: they never leave a complex idea as a wall of text. Every important point gets a visual framework that makes it scannable in seconds.

The goal is always the same: a good consultant can hand someone the deck, walk out of the room, and the argument still holds — because the visuals carry the story.

### The Visual-Text Balance Rule

For every slide, ask: **"Can someone understand the main point in 10 seconds without reading everything?"** If the answer is no, the slide needs a visual framework.

A rough balance to aim for:
- **Executive summary / recommendation slides**: 60% visual structure (boxes, columns, callout), 40% text
- **Data / evidence slides**: 70% chart or visual element, 30% annotation and title
- **Process / roadmap slides**: 80% diagram or timeline, 20% labels
- **Conclusion / action slides**: 50/50 — clear headline + structured next steps table

### Clarity Frameworks Catalog

Choose the right framework for each slide's purpose:

**For summarizing complex arguments:**

| Framework | What it looks like | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **3-Box Summary** | Three side-by-side boxes: Situação → Problema → Solução | Executive summaries, opening slides |
| **Key Messages Card** | 3-4 insight cards with icon + bold headline + 1-line explanation | Closing summaries, board takeaways |
| **Governing Thought Pyramid** | Big bold statement at top, 3 supporting bullets below with icons | Recommendation slides |

**For showing data and evidence:**

| Framework | What it looks like | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Evidence Sandwich** | Claim headline (top) → Chart (middle) → "So what" callout box (bottom) | Any slide with one key data point |
| **Big Number Callout** | One large number (60-72pt) + context label below + supporting bullets | KPIs, growth figures, impact stats |
| **Before / After Split** | Left column = current state, Right column = future state | Change narratives, proposals |
| **Waterfall** | Bar chart showing starting value → components → ending value | Revenue/cost decomposition |

**For structuring options and decisions:**

| Framework | What it looks like | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **2x2 Matrix** | Four quadrants with options plotted across two axes | Prioritization, positioning, tradeoffs |
| **3-Option Comparison** | Three columns, each with pros/cons/cost, one highlighted as recommended | Strategic choices |
| **Decision Tree** | Branching diagram with decision points and outcomes | Risk analysis, scenario planning |

**For showing process and sequence:**

| Framework | What it looks like | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase Timeline** | Horizontal arrows with named phases and key milestones | Implementation roadmaps |
| **Numbered Steps** | 3-5 numbered steps with icon + title + brief description | Process explanation, how-it-works |
| **Circular Loop** | Continuous cycle diagram | Feedback loops, operating models |

### The Mandatory Synthesis Slide

Every presentation must include at least one **Synthesis Slide** — a single slide that could stand alone as the entire deck if needed. Think of it as the "if you only see one slide, this is it."

What it contains:
- The governing thought or recommendation (1 bold sentence)
- The 3 key supporting points (each as a visual card or icon row)
- A clear indication of what the audience should do next

This is typically placed as the Executive Summary (slide 2) and/or repeated as the closing slide. For board presentations, this is often the only slide that actually gets discussed.

## Step 4 — Write the Slide Blueprint

Produce a complete **slide-by-slide blueprint** before building anything. This is the most important step — it forces you to think clearly about structure before design.

For each slide, define:

- **Action Title** (see below)
- **Key Message** (1 sentence — what the audience must take away)
- **Visual Framework** (which framework from the catalog above, and why)
- **Supporting Content** (data, arguments, examples — be specific)
- **Speaker note (optional)** (what to say that's not on the slide)

### The Action Title Rule (McKinsey/BCG standard)

Every slide title must be an **action title** — a complete sentence that states the insight, not just labels the topic.

❌ Bad (topic titles): "Revenue," "Market Share," "Key Risks"
✅ Good (action titles): "Revenue cresceu 40% impulsionado por novos clientes no Sul," "Nossa participação de mercado caiu 3pp em 12 meses," "Três riscos ameaçam a entrega até o Q3"

The test: if you covered up the slide content and only read the title, would you know what the slide proves? If yes, it's a good action title.

### Slide Blueprint Template

```
Slide N — [ACTION TITLE]
Key message: [one sentence]
Visual framework: [name from catalog + brief description of how it looks]
Content: [specific data, arguments, text to include]
Speaker note (optional): [what to say that's not on the slide]
```

---

## Step 5 — Narrative Coherence Check

Before handing off to the pptx skill, do a "narrative audit":

1. Read only the action titles, in order. Does it tell a coherent story? If you removed all content and only had titles, would the argument still hold?
2. Does every slide support the governing thought / recommendation?
3. Is there any slide that exists "just because we have this data"? Cut it or reframe it to serve the argument.
4. Does the first slide (or executive summary) answer the question "so what should we do / believe"?

The test: **"So what?" after every slide.** If you can't answer why this slide matters to the audience's decision, rework it.

## Step 6 — Visual Guidance

Since the user has their own template, suggest adjustments to make it more consultant-grade without breaking their identity.

### Layout recommendations by slide type:

| Slide Type | Recommended Layout |
|---|---|
| Title / cover | Full-bleed background, large headline, minimal text |
| Executive summary | 3-column "situation / complication / resolution" or 3 key bullets + recommendation callout box |
| Data/insight slide | Big number callout (60-72pt) + 2-3 supporting context lines, or chart left + interpretation right |
| Comparison / options | 2-3 column comparison with a clear "winner" highlighted |
| Process / roadmap | Timeline or numbered steps with clear phases |
| Recommendation | Bold headline + 3 supporting arguments as icon rows |
| Next steps | Simple table: action / owner / deadline |

### Consulting visual principles (suggest to user when relevant):

- **Whitespace is credibility.** Crowded slides signal panic; clean slides signal confidence. Use ≥ 40% whitespace per slide.
- **One idea per slide.** If a slide is trying to say two things, it's actually two slides.
- **Data annotation.** Never put a chart without a callout highlighting the key insight. The chart is evidence; the annotation is the argument.
- **Consistent visual hierarchy.** Action title (largest) → supporting data → labels/footnotes. Don't let footnotes compete with the title.
- **Color = signal, not decoration.** Use your brand colors, but add a single accent color (e.g., red or orange) only for the most important element on a given slide.

## Quick Reference: Consulting Slide Archetypes

Use these proven slide patterns from top consulting firms:

| Archetype | When to use | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **Governing Thought** | First content slide after title | Bold headline answering "what should we do?" + 3 supporting arguments below |
| **Waterfall** | Show change over time or decomposition | Bar chart showing starting point → components → end point |
| **2x2 Matrix** | Position options across two dimensions | Quadrant with options plotted; label each quadrant |
| **Stacked Argument** | Build a multi-part case | Slide for each argument, all titled to support one governing thought |
| **Evidence Sandwich** | Support a claim with data | Claim (top) → Chart/data (middle) → So-what interpretation (bottom) |
| **So What Summary** | Close a section | Restate the key finding in one crisp sentence; next steps or implications |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Burying the lead**: Don't make the audience wait until slide 10 to understand your recommendation.
- **Data dumps**: A slide full of numbers without interpretation is noise, not insight.
- **Consensus titles**: "Overview," "Background," "Introduction" — these waste a slide title. Every title should do work.
- **"And then" structure**: A good presentation isn't "first this happened, then that, then this." It's "here's the answer, and here's why."
- **Presenter-first thinking**: The deck is not for you to follow during the meeting. It's a leave-behind argument. Make it self-standing.
